# Pitch Review

Audit any pitch, proposal, email, sales message, or presentation against the Pitch Anything framework. Identify weaknesses and provide specific rewrites that shift the content from needy/analytical to commanding/compelling.

## Review Process

When the user provides content to review, run it through all five audit layers below. For each issue found, provide the specific problematic text, explain WHY it's a problem using the framework, and provide a concrete rewrite.

## Audit Layer 1: Neediness Scan

Neediness is the #1 deal killer. It's validation-seeking behavior that signals weakness to the audience's croc brain. Scan the content for these deadly patterns:

**Explicit neediness signals (flag immediately):**
- "What do you think?" or "Do you think this is good?"
- "I hope this works for you" or "I hope you'll consider"
- "We'd love the opportunity to..." or "We'd be grateful for the chance to..."
- "Please let me know if you have any questions"
- "Just checking in" or "Just following up"
- "We can be flexible on..." (when not asked)
- "We can sign right away if you want us to"
- "Let me know what works for your schedule"
- "Sorry to bother you" or "Sorry for the follow-up"
- Any sentence that asks the reader to validate the sender

**Implicit neediness signals (flag and explain):**
- Excessive qualifiers: "maybe," "perhaps," "I think," "sort of," "kind of"
- Apologetic tone anywhere in the content
- Offering discounts or concessions unprompted
- Multiple exclamation points signaling over-eagerness
- Explaining why you're worthy instead of demonstrating it
- Providing more information than asked for (over-justifying)
- Following up multiple times without adding new value
- Letting the other party set all the terms, timeline, and format

**Rewrite principle:** Replace validation-seeking with the Tao of Steve:
1. Eliminate desires. Want nothing.
2. Be excellent. Show one thing you do well.
3. Withdraw. Pull away when expected to chase.

**Example:**
- BEFORE: "I'd love the opportunity to discuss how we might be able to help your team. Please let me know if you have 15 minutes this week?"
- AFTER: "We're solving [specific problem] for companies like [peer company]. I have 15 minutes Thursday at 2pm. Does that work?"

## Audit Layer 2: Frame Analysis

Determine who holds the frame in the content. The person who holds the frame controls the interaction.

**Signs the sender has LOST the frame:**
- Reacting to the recipient's priorities instead of setting their own
- Using the recipient's language/framing instead of introducing new framing
- Defensive explanations ("The reason we..." "To clarify...")
- Accommodating without being asked
- Positioning themselves below the recipient
- Explaining their authority or credentials at length (if you have to explain it, you don't have it)

**Signs the sender HOLDS the frame:**
- Setting the agenda, timeline, and terms
- Making the recipient react to their statements
- Confident assertions without qualifiers
- Prize positioning (the recipient should want to work with them)
- Time constraints that signal demand

**Frame check questions:**
- Who is reacting to whom in this content?
- Is the sender positioned as the prize or the commodity?
- Does the content make the recipient qualify themselves, or does the sender qualify themselves?
- Are there any beta traps the sender has walked into?

**Rewrite principle:** Flip the frame. The sender should be the prize. The recipient should be qualifying themselves.

**Example:**
- BEFORE: "We have extensive experience in this space and believe we could add significant value to your organization."
- AFTER: "We're selective about partnerships. Based on what I've seen, there could be a strong fit here, but I'd need to understand more about how your team operates."

## Audit Layer 3: Tension and Attention

A pitch without tension is a pitch without interest. Scan for the absence of the attention cocktail (dopamine + norepinephrine).

**Red flags -- content is flat if it:**
- Reads as a list of features or facts with no narrative arc
- Contains no push/pull dynamics
- Never introduces uncertainty or risk
- Never takes anything away from the reader
- Is entirely positive with no tension
- Could be a Wikipedia article (informational, no emotional stakes)
- Front-loads all the good stuff with nothing held back

**What to add:**

Push/Pull loops at the appropriate intensity:
- Low: "There's a real possibility we might not be right for each other. But if this works out, the combined force could be remarkable."
- Medium: "If we don't share the same values around [X], this would never work. But obviously you take [X] seriously."
- High: "Based on [signal], this might not be the right fit. But I'd rather find that out now."

Novelty injections:
- Unexpected metaphors or comparisons
- Counterintuitive data points
- Pattern breaks in tone or structure
- Something the reader hasn't heard before

Intrigue elements:
- A personal story with risk, time pressure, and unresolved tension
- Information gaps that create curiosity
- Stakes that make the outcome matter

**Example:**
- BEFORE: "Our platform integrates with 50+ tools, offers real-time analytics, and has been adopted by 200 enterprise customers."
- AFTER: "When [company] tried to solve this last year, they burned through $2M before calling us. I'll tell you what happened -- but first, the thing that matters: we built something that their existing 50+ tools couldn't replicate."

## Audit Layer 4: Hot vs. Cold Cognition Check

People decide they want something BEFORE they understand it. Brain scans show decisions form up to 7 seconds before conscious awareness. Most data is collected to justify decisions, not make them.

**Scan for cold cognition traps:**
- Leading with data, statistics, or technical specifications
- ROI calculations as the primary argument
- Feature comparison tables
- Lengthy technical explanations
- Anything that activates the reader's analytical brain before activating desire
- Logic-first structure (problem > data > solution > ask)

**The fix:** Content should activate hot cognitions FIRST, then provide cold cognitions as justification.

**Hot cognition triggers to add:**
1. Intrigue: something they want but can't have yet
2. Prize positioning: "I'm choosy about who I work with"
3. Time constraint: "This needs to happen by [date]"
4. Moral authority: "We do things right. Can you?"

**Restructure principle:** Emotion first, logic second. The croc brain must say "yes" before the neocortex gets to analyze.

**Example:**
- BEFORE: "Our solution reduces processing time by 47%, saves an average of $340K annually, and integrates with your existing tech stack. Here's the ROI breakdown..."
- AFTER: "Three of your competitors signed with us last quarter. I can't share names yet, but the results changed how they operate. The question isn't whether the numbers work -- they do. The question is whether you're ready to move this fast."

## Audit Layer 5: Structural Timing Check

For longer pitches and presentations, check the timing structure against the 20-minute framework.

**Optimal structure:**
- Minutes 0-2: Set time frame and context
- Minutes 2-4: Track record (one great thing, not a resume)
- Minutes 4-5: "Why Now?" with three converging forces (economic, social, technology)
- Minutes 5-6: Big idea introduction using the pattern template
- Minutes 6-15: Budget, secret sauce, competition with tension loops woven in
- Minutes 15-17: Clear deal terms (what, when, how)
- Minutes 17-20: Four-frame hot cognition stack (intrigue, prize, time, moral authority)
- After 20: Anti-neediness close

**Common structural problems:**
- No time frame set at the opening (audience doesn't know how long they're trapped)
- Background section too long (more than 2 minutes / more than one accomplishment)
- No "Why Now?" section (idea feels timeless instead of urgent)
- Tension loops missing from the middle section
- No hot cognition stack at the end
- Closing asks for validation instead of withdrawing

## Output Format

For every review, deliver:

1. **Overall assessment:** One paragraph on the content's biggest strength and biggest weakness
2. **Issue list:** Each issue with the specific text, the framework violation, and a rewrite
3. **Rewritten version:** The full content rewritten with all fixes applied
4. **Score card** (rate 1-10 on each dimension):
   - Neediness: 1 = desperate, 10 = completely non-needy
   - Frame ownership: 1 = fully reactive, 10 = fully in control
   - Tension/attention: 1 = flat, 10 = compelling push/pull throughout
   - Hot cognition activation: 1 = purely analytical, 10 = desire-first
   - Structure: 1 = no framework, 10 = perfect 20-min structure (if applicable)

## Golden Rules (Apply to Every Review)

1. Humor and fun are non-negotiable. Defiance without humor feels hostile. Defiance WITH humor creates attraction.
2. Plowing: the content should always move forward. No self-doubt, no hedging, no circling back.
3. If the content explains the sender's authority at length, they don't have the frame.
4. Hot cognitions beat cold cognitions every time. Emotion first, logic second.
5. Novelty is oxygen for attention. Flag anything that sounds generic or templated.
6. Always Be Leaving, never Always Be Closing.
7. The most important deliverable in any deal is the person, not the product.
8. Money is a commodity. It's available in a thousand places. There is only one of the sender.
