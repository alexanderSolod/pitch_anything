# Anti-Patterns Reference

Detailed library of common mistakes in pitches, proposals, and professional communication, with framework explanations and rewrites. Read this when reviewing content and you need more examples of specific anti-patterns.

## Neediness Anti-Patterns

### The Grateful Opener
BEFORE: "Thank you so much for taking the time to meet with us today. We really appreciate the opportunity."
WHY IT'S BAD: Positions the sender as beneath the recipient. Signals that the recipient's time is more valuable.
AFTER: "Good to see you. I've got about 20 minutes before my next meeting -- let's get into it."

### The Eager Closer
BEFORE: "We'd love to move forward whenever you're ready. We can be flexible on timing and terms."
WHY IT'S BAD: "Love to move forward" = wanting. "Whenever you're ready" = they set the pace. "Flexible on terms" = unprompted concession.
AFTER: "We're committing resources to this in Q2. If you want in, let's lock terms by Friday."

### The Apologetic Follow-Up
BEFORE: "Sorry to follow up again -- just wanted to check if you had a chance to review the proposal?"
WHY IT'S BAD: "Sorry" = neediness. "Just wanted to check" = validation-seeking. The question mark at the end turns a statement into a plea.
AFTER: "We're finalizing partner commitments this week. Let me know by Thursday if you want to be part of it."

### The Over-Explainer
BEFORE: "The reason I'm reaching out is that I noticed your company recently expanded into the European market, and given our extensive experience with European regulatory frameworks, I thought there might be a natural synergy between our capabilities and your growth objectives."
WHY IT'S BAD: Justifying why you're contacting them = explaining your frame. If you have to explain why someone should talk to you, you've already lost the frame.
AFTER: "You're expanding into Europe. We've navigated the regulatory landscape for three companies your size in the last year. Worth a 15-minute conversation?"

### The Discount Volunteer
BEFORE: "We could also look at a reduced scope to fit your budget if needed."
WHY IT'S BAD: Offering concessions before being asked signals desperation and commoditizes your offering.
AFTER: Remove entirely. If they ask about budget, say: "Our pricing reflects the results we deliver. Let's talk about what success looks like first."

## Frame Loss Anti-Patterns

### The Reactive Proposal
BEFORE: "Per your request, here is the proposal following the format you specified with the sections you outlined."
WHY IT'S BAD: Every element of this is reactive. Their request, their format, their sections. You're operating entirely within their frame.
AFTER: "Here's our proposal. We've structured it around the three things that actually determine whether this works." Then use YOUR structure.

### The Authority Explainer
BEFORE: "With over 15 years of experience in the industry and having worked with Fortune 500 companies including [list], we have the expertise and track record to deliver results."
WHY IT'S BAD: If you have to explain your authority, you don't have the frame. This reads as "please believe I'm qualified."
AFTER: "When [impressive client] had this exact problem, we fixed it in 6 weeks. Here's what we'd do for you."

### The Committee Accommodator
BEFORE: "We're happy to present to whoever you think should be involved. Just let us know the best time and we'll work around your schedule."
WHY IT'S BAD: Handing over all power dynamics. They choose the audience, the time, the format. Classic beta trap acceptance.
AFTER: "I'd like to present to whoever makes the final call. I have Thursday at 2 or Friday at 10. Which works?"

### The Scope Creep Acceptor
BEFORE: "Sure, we can definitely add that to the scope. We want to make sure you get everything you need."
WHY IT'S BAD: Accepting scope changes without renegotiation signals that your boundaries are soft and your pricing was inflated.
AFTER: "We can do that. It changes the scope, so let me send you revised terms by end of day."

## Flat Content Anti-Patterns

### The Feature List
BEFORE: "Our platform offers: real-time analytics, 50+ integrations, custom dashboards, team collaboration tools, automated reporting, and 24/7 support."
WHY IT'S BAD: Zero tension. Zero narrative. The croc brain filters this as "boring information" and stops listening.
AFTER: "Most analytics platforms give you dashboards. We give you decisions. One client caught a $2M revenue leak in their first week that their previous tools completely missed."

### The Positive-Only Pitch
BEFORE: "This is a tremendous opportunity with massive market potential and incredible team dynamics. Everything is perfectly aligned for explosive growth."
WHY IT'S BAD: No tension = no attention. The brain needs norepinephrine (triggered by threat of loss) alongside dopamine (triggered by reward). All-positive content flatlines attention.
AFTER: "The market window is open right now, but it won't stay open. Two well-funded competitors are 6 months behind us. The question is whether we capitalize on the lead or let it evaporate."

### The Data Dump Opener
BEFORE: "The global market for X is projected to reach $47B by 2028, growing at a 12.3% CAGR. The TAM for our specific segment is $8.2B with a serviceable market of $2.1B."
WHY IT'S BAD: Leading with cold cognition. The analytical brain activates, and once it's running, it's very hard to switch to emotional engagement. You've lost the croc brain.
AFTER: "Three forces are converging right now that make this the moment: [economic force], [social force], [technology force]. The market is moving. We're positioned at the center."

### The Wikipedia Section
BEFORE: "Artificial intelligence has been advancing rapidly in recent years. Machine learning models have become increasingly sophisticated, enabling new applications across industries. Our company leverages these advances to..."
WHY IT'S BAD: The brain stops paying attention to anything it thinks it already knows. Generic context = novelty death.
AFTER: Cut entirely. Start with what's unique about your approach. If you need context, make it surprising: "Everyone thinks AI is about automation. They're wrong. The real shift is [unexpected angle]."

## Structural Anti-Patterns

### The Resume Opening
BEFORE: "Founded in 2018, our team brings together expertise from Google, McKinsey, and Stanford. Our CEO has 20 years of experience in..."
WHY IT'S BAD: People's impression is based on the AVERAGE of your accomplishments, not the sum. Five items averages lower than one great item. Also: this is cold cognition territory.
AFTER: "When our CEO built [one impressive thing], it changed how [industry] works. That's the same approach we're applying here."

### The No-Time-Frame Opening
BEFORE: "Thanks for having us. We've prepared a comprehensive presentation covering our background, market analysis, product overview, financial projections, and go-to-market strategy."
WHY IT'S BAD: The audience now expects a long, exhaustive presentation. Their croc brain says: "Danger, I'm trapped." Attention drops before you start.
AFTER: "I've got 20 minutes to give you the big idea, then we'll talk. Let's go."

### The Missing "Why Now?"
BEFORE: [Pitch goes straight from background to product description]
WHY IT'S BAD: Without "Why Now?", the idea feels timeless, which means there's no urgency. The croc brain doesn't detect motion, so it doesn't alert.
AFTER: Insert three converging forces (economic, social, technology) that show why this idea is inevitable RIGHT NOW.

### The Validation-Seeking Close
BEFORE: "So that's our pitch. What do you think? Do you have any questions? We'd love to hear your feedback."
WHY IT'S BAD: Three neediness signals in a row. "What do you think" hands the frame to the audience. "Love to hear your feedback" positions you as the supplicant.
AFTER: "That's the opportunity. We're committing fully in [timeframe] with or without outside participation. The question is whether you want to be part of it. Tell me about your team -- I need to understand if this is the right fit."

## Email-Specific Anti-Patterns

### The Subject Line That Begs
BEFORE: "Quick question" or "Touching base" or "Following up (3rd time)"
AFTER: "Q2 partnership -- decision needed by Friday" or "[Company] + [their company]: the pitch" or "[Specific result] in [timeframe]"

### The Wall of Text
BEFORE: [6 paragraphs of background, context, and justification before getting to the point]
WHY IT'S BAD: Nobody reads this. The croc brain sees density and filters it out.
AFTER: 3-5 sentences max for cold outreach. Get to the point in sentence one. Use whitespace.

### The Multiple Questions Email
BEFORE: "Would you be interested in learning more? If so, when works for you? And should I include anyone else from your team?"
WHY IT'S BAD: Each question is a decision. Multiple decisions = cognitive load = inaction. Also, every question after the first dilutes urgency.
AFTER: "I have Thursday at 2pm. Worth 15 minutes?"
