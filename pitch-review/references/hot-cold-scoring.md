# Hot vs. Cold Cognition Scoring Guide

Use this reference when scoring content on the hot/cold cognition dimension and when restructuring content to lead with emotion.

## The Core Science

People decide they like or want something BEFORE they understand it. Brain scans show decisions form up to 7 seconds before conscious awareness. Data is collected after the fact to justify decisions, not to make them.

This means: if your content leads with data, you're feeding the justification engine before the decision engine has even fired. The croc brain needs to say "I want this" before the neocortex gets to ask "Does this make sense?"

## Hot Cognitions (Desire-Based)

Hot cognitions are fast, emotional, automatic responses. They're triggered by:
- Intrigue (something you want but can't have yet)
- Social proof from people you respect
- Scarcity / time pressure
- Status / prize dynamics
- Narrative with personal stakes
- Novelty / the unexpected
- Moral resonance (shared values)

## Cold Cognitions (Analysis-Based)

Cold cognitions are slow, deliberate, calculated responses. They're triggered by:
- Statistics and data points
- ROI calculations
- Feature comparisons
- Technical specifications
- Market size numbers
- Financial projections
- Process descriptions

## The Critical Rule

Humans cannot have hot and cold cognitions at the same time. Once the analytical brain activates, it is very difficult to switch back to emotional engagement.

This means: cold cognition content early in a pitch is almost impossible to recover from. You've trained the audience's brain to analyze, and now everything that follows gets analyzed instead of desired.

## Scoring Guide

**Score 1-2: Fully Analytical**
Content leads with data. No narrative. No emotional hooks. Reads like a research report or product spec sheet. The croc brain would filter this out within 30 seconds.

**Score 3-4: Analysis-Heavy with Some Emotion**
Content is primarily data-driven but includes occasional emotional language or a brief story. The emotional elements feel bolted on rather than structural.

**Score 5-6: Mixed**
Content alternates between analytical and emotional elements. Neither dominates. The structure doesn't clearly lead with desire.

**Score 7-8: Emotion-Led with Supporting Data**
Content opens with narrative, intrigue, or social proof. Data appears after emotional engagement is established. The audience wants the thing before they understand the numbers.

**Score 9-10: Full Hot Cognition Architecture**
Content follows the four-frame stack: intrigue creates curiosity, prize frame creates desire to qualify, time frame creates urgency, moral authority creates deep wanting. Cold data appears only as validation for a decision the audience has already emotionally made.

## Restructuring Template

When content scores below 7, restructure using this sequence:

1. **Open with intrigue or a personal story** (hot)
   - A real situation with stakes, risk, and unresolved tension
   - Something the reader wants to know but you haven't told them yet

2. **Establish prize dynamics** (hot)
   - Position the sender as selective, in-demand, or hard to get
   - Make the reader feel they need to qualify

3. **Introduce the idea with minimum threat** (warm)
   - Use the Idea Introduction Pattern: For [target] who are dissatisfied with [current], my product is [category] that provides [solution]
   - Keep it simple. No jargon. No complexity.

4. **Build tension with push/pull loops** (hot)
   - "We might not be right for each other... but if this works..."
   - Alternate between creating and resolving tension

5. **NOW add data as justification** (cold, but supportive)
   - Revenue, margins, market size, projections
   - This feels like validation for a desire they already have
   - Keep it high-level. Details in the appendix.

6. **Close with time frame and moral authority** (hot)
   - Real deadline. Fair but firm.
   - Values alignment check.

## The Anti-Neediness Check

After restructuring for hot cognition, do one final pass for neediness:

**Remove any sentence that:**
- Asks the reader to validate the sender
- Uses "hope," "would love," "just," "maybe"
- Offers unprompted concessions
- Apologizes for taking up time or following up
- Positions the sender as grateful for the opportunity

**Replace with sentences that:**
- State facts without seeking approval
- Set terms rather than asking for them
- Create scarcity around the sender's time and attention
- Make the reader qualify themselves
- End with withdrawal, not pursuit

## The Tao of Steve (Anti-Neediness Formula)

Named after the philosophy of the "coolest Steves" (McQueen, McGarrett, Austin):

1. Eliminate your desires. Want nothing. Let things come to you.
2. Be excellent in the presence of others. Show one thing you do well.
3. Withdraw. At the crucial moment when people expect you to come after them, pull away.

In written content, this translates to:
1. Never ask for what you want directly. State what's available and let them come to you.
2. Lead with one impressive proof point, not a list of credentials.
3. Close by pulling away: "If this isn't the right time, no worries. We'll be fully committed by [date] regardless."
