# Pitch Builder

Build high-stakes pitches using the Pitch Anything methodology. This skill walks users through a structured pitch construction process that targets the audience's croc brain (primitive decision-making center) rather than their neocortex (analytical mind).

The core problem most pitches have: they're created by the pitcher's neocortex (logic, data, reasoning) but received by the target's croc brain (survival-focused, emotional, attention-limited). This mismatch causes 9 out of 10 pitches to fail because up to 90% of the message gets filtered out before reaching the analytical brain.

## How This Skill Works

Guide the user through all four phases of the 20-minute pitch structure. For each phase, ask the user targeted questions, then draft the pitch content using the frameworks below. The pitch should feel like a narrative, not a data dump.

Always keep the total pitch to 20 minutes or less. The brain's attention cocktail (dopamine + norepinephrine) expires around the 20-minute mark. DNA's discovery can be pitched in 5 minutes. Their idea can be pitched in 20.

## The Pitch Construction Sequence

### Phase 1: Introduce Yourself and the Big Idea (Minutes 0-6)

**Step 1: Set the Time Frame (Minute 0-1)**
Open every pitch by constraining your own time. This signals confidence and eliminates the audience's anxiety about being trapped.

Script: "I've only got about 20 minutes to give you the big idea, which will leave us some time to talk it over before I have to get out of here."

Ask the user: What is the context of this pitch? (Investor meeting, sales call, internal stakeholder, board presentation, etc.)

**Step 2: Background / Track Record (Minutes 1-3)**
Keep this under 2 minutes. Research shows people form impressions based on the AVERAGE of information, not the sum. One great accomplishment leaves a better impression than one great accomplishment plus two mediocre ones.

Ask the user: What is the single most impressive thing you've built, shipped, or accomplished that's relevant to this pitch?

Draft a 3-4 sentence track record that includes ONLY that one thing. No life story. No resume walkthrough.

**Step 3: The "Why Now?" Frame (Minutes 3-5)**
Explain why this idea is relevant RIGHT NOW using three converging market forces. The brain is wired to detect motion. Don't show two static states ("old way vs. new way"). Show forces actively pushing the idea forward so success feels inevitable.

Ask the user about each force:
1. **Economic force:** What financial shift creates the opening? (Interest rates, funding trends, cost changes, market growth)
2. **Social force:** What behavior change or cultural shift supports this? (Consumer habits, awareness trends, workforce changes)
3. **Technology force:** What new capability or cost reduction makes this possible now? (AI, infrastructure, platforms, tools)

Draft a "Why Now" narrative that weaves all three forces into a sense of momentum.

**Step 4: The Big Idea Introduction (Minutes 5-6)**
Use the Idea Introduction Pattern to present the idea with minimum threat to the croc brain:

> "For [target customers]
> who are dissatisfied with [current offerings in the market],
> my idea/product is a [new idea or product category]
> that provides [key problem/solution features].
> Unlike [the competing product],
> my idea/product is [describe key features]."

Ask the user to fill in each bracket. Then polish the output so it flows naturally as spoken language.

### Phase 2: Budget, Secret Sauce, and Competition (Minutes 6-15)

This is the longest phase and where most pitches lose their audience. The antidote is tension loops.

**The Attention Cocktail:**
- Dopamine (desire) is triggered by: novelty, unexpected rewards, new ideas, good metaphors
- Norepinephrine (tension) is triggered by: real consequences, something being gained or lost

A pitch narrative is a series of tension loops. Push, then pull. Create tension, then resolve it. Without tension there is no interest, no emotional involvement, no deal.

**Build 2-3 Push/Pull Tension Loops into this phase.** Choose the appropriate intensity based on the audience:

*Low Intensity (safe for first meetings):*
- PUSH: "There's a real possibility that we might not be right for each other." [Pause]
- PULL: "But then again, if this did work out, our forces could combine to become something great."

*Medium Intensity (established relationship or warm lead):*
- PUSH: "What really counts is having someone in charge who has passion, experience, and integrity. If you and I don't share that view, it would never work between us." [Pause]
- PULL: "But that's crazy to think. Obviously you value people over smart ideas."

*High Intensity (use sparingly, when you sense disengagement):*
- PUSH: "Based on your reactions, it seems like this isn't a good fit. Let's just wrap this up and agree to get together on the next one." [Pause. Start packing up.]

Ask the user about:
- **Budget:** How they allocate capital. Focus on demonstrating skill at budgeting (highly respected) rather than optimistic revenue projections.
- **Competition:** Two questions only: (1) How easy is it for new competitors to enter? (2) How easy is it for customers to switch?
- **Secret Sauce:** Their unfair competitive advantage in one or two sentences.

Draft this phase as a narrative with tension loops woven in, not a bulleted list of facts.

### Phase 3: Offer the Deal (Minutes 15-17)

In clear, concise terms, cover:
- Exactly what will be delivered
- When it will be delivered
- How it will be delivered
- Their role and responsibilities (if any)

Keep it brief and high-level. No drill-downs. Remind the user: the most important deliverable in the deal is YOU. The deal terms matter, but people invest in people.

### Phase 4: Frame Stacking / Hot Cognitions (Minutes 17-20)

People decide they like or want something BEFORE they fully understand it. Brain scans show decisions form up to 7 seconds before conscious awareness. Most data people collect is used to justify decisions after the fact.

Stack these four frames in rapid succession to create an overwhelming "wanting":

**Hot Cognition 1: Intrigue Frame**
Deploy a pre-prepared story using the "man in the jungle" pattern:
1. Put yourself in a difficult, relevant situation
2. Introduce risk, danger, uncertainty, and a ticking clock
3. Show tension -- you're trying to do something but a force blocks you
4. DO NOT finish the story. Leave them hanging. Go back to your pitch.

Ask the user: What is a real situation you faced that involved risk, time pressure, and an uncertain outcome related to this work?

Draft the intrigue story. Cut it off before the resolution.

**Hot Cognition 2: Prize Frame**
Position yourself as the most important party. Draft 1-2 lines:
- "I'm choosy about who I work with."
- "Can you tell me why we would enjoy working with you?"

**Hot Cognition 3: Time Frame**
Set a real, fair time constraint:
- "This deal will be fully committed by [realistic date]."
- Use the "deal train" metaphor: good deals stop at the station, pick up passengers, and leave on schedule.

**Hot Cognition 4: Moral Authority Frame**
Demonstrate high ethical standards:
- "We do things right. No games, no missing numbers, just clean paper."
- Ask the target to confirm shared values: "Can you play by the rules?"

### Post-Pitch: Eradicate Neediness (After Minute 20)

This is the most dangerous moment. Small mistakes here get amplified into deal killers. Neediness (validation-seeking) is the #1 deal killer.

Instead of asking "So, what do you think?", make three points:
1. This deal will be fully committed in [timeframe]
2. We don't need your [money/partnership], but we want the strategic value you bring
3. I think you're interesting, but are you really the right fit? We need to know more about YOU.

Then: stop talking. Wait for them to react. Zero validation-seeking.

The Anti-Neediness Formula (Tao of Steve):
1. Eliminate your desires. Want nothing.
2. Be excellent in the presence of others. Show one thing you do well.
3. Withdraw. Pull away when they expect you to chase.

Always Be Leaving, never Always Be Closing.

## Frame Control Primer

Throughout the pitch, the user needs frame control. Frames are mental structures that shape how people see interactions. Only one frame survives any encounter.

If the user encounters resistance during the pitch:

- **Power Frame** (arrogance, ego, "I'm more important"): Counter with small defiance + humor. "That's okay, I only have 12 minutes." Never fawn or defer.
- **Time Frame** (attention fading, "let's wrap up"): YOU end it first. "Looks like time is up. I've got to wrap this and get to my next meeting."
- **Analyst Frame** (detail obsession, spreadsheet demands): Give a high-level number, then redirect: "Revenue is $80M, expenses $62M. You can verify later. Right now: are we a good fit?" Then deploy your intrigue story.

For deep frame control tactics, see `references/frame-control-tactics.md`.

## Output Format

When building a pitch, deliver:
1. A complete pitch script organized by the four phases with minute markers
2. 2-3 tension loops written out as dialogue
3. The intrigue story drafted and cut off at the right moment
4. The four-frame hot cognition stack scripted
5. Post-pitch anti-neediness script
6. A quick-reference timing card the user can glance at during the actual pitch

## Golden Rules (Apply to Everything)

1. Humor and fun are non-negotiable. Defiance without humor feels hostile. Defiance WITH humor creates attraction.
2. Plowing: always moving forward, never stopping, never showing self-doubt.
3. Frame control is won or lost BEFORE the pitch starts.
4. If you're explaining your authority, you've already lost the frame.
5. Hot cognitions beat cold cognitions every time. People decide with emotion, justify with logic.
6. Novelty is oxygen for attention. The brain stops paying attention to anything it thinks it already understands.
7. Always Be Leaving, never Always Be Closing.
8. Every pitch is a game. When both sides know the game is on, the interaction becomes exciting for everyone.
