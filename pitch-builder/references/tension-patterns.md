# Tension Patterns and Hot Cognition Reference

Detailed patterns for creating and maintaining attention through dopamine/norepinephrine cycling.

## The Attention Cocktail

Attention is a chemical process, not a choice. Two chemicals drive it:

- **Dopamine** (desire): triggered by novelty, unexpected rewards, new ideas, good metaphors, visual stimuli
- **Norepinephrine** (tension): triggered by real consequences, something being gained or lost

To create desire, offer a reward. To create tension, take something away.

This cocktail works for approximately 20 minutes before the chemicals fall out of balance. This is why no pitch should exceed 20 minutes.

## Push/Pull Tension Loop Library

Every pitch narrative should contain 2-3 tension loops. Each loop has a PUSH (create tension by taking something away) and a PULL (resolve tension by offering something back).

### Low Intensity
Best for: first meetings, cautious audiences, early in the pitch

PUSH: "There's a real possibility that we might not be right for each other." [Pause]
PULL: "But then again, if this did work out, our forces could combine to become something great."

### Medium Intensity
Best for: warm leads, established relationships, mid-pitch when you feel good engagement

PUSH: "What really counts is having someone in charge who has passion, experience, and integrity. If you and I don't share that view, it would never work between us." [Pause]
PULL: "But that's crazy to think. Obviously you value people over smart ideas."

### High Intensity
Best for: when you sense disengagement, when the audience is drifting, as a last resort

PUSH: "Based on your reactions, it seems like this isn't a good fit. Let's just wrap this up and agree to get together on the next one." [Pause. Start packing up. Be willing to leave.]

The pull in a high-intensity loop comes from the audience, not from you. They will either let you leave (in which case the deal was dead anyway) or they will re-engage ("Wait, no, let's keep going").

### Custom Loop Construction
To build a custom loop:
1. PUSH: identify something the audience values about this deal, then threaten to remove it
2. PAUSE: let the tension sit for 2-3 seconds minimum
3. PULL: reintroduce the value with a conditional ("but if...")

## The Four-Frame Hot Cognition Stack

Stack these in Phase 4 (minutes 17-20) in rapid succession. The goal is to shut down cold analytical processes and activate croc-brain "wanting."

### Frame 1: Intrigue
- Introduce something the target wants but cannot get right now
- Tell your intrigue story using the "man in the jungle" pattern
- Characters face real obstacles
- DO NOT finish the story

### Frame 2: Prize
- Position yourself as the most important party
- "I'm choosy about who I work with"
- "Can you tell me why we would enjoy working with you?"
- Internal mantra: I am the prize. You are trying to impress me.

### Frame 3: Time
- Set a real, fair constraint: "We need to decide by [date]."
- "Good deals are like an Amtrak train. They stop at the station, pick up investors, and leave on schedule."
- Don't use extreme pressure (feels cheap and forced)

### Frame 4: Moral Authority
- The most powerful frame (stronger than money, status, power)
- "We do things right. No games, no missing numbers, just clean paper."
- Ask the target to confirm shared values: "Can you play by the rules?"
- Triggers the deepest wanting because the target needs to prove worthiness

When all four stack correctly: cold analysis shuts down. The croc brain takes over, creating a "wanting" that drives the deal forward.

## Novelty Techniques

The brain stops paying attention to anything it thinks it already understands. Novelty is oxygen for attention.

Ways to inject novelty mid-pitch:
- Unexpected metaphors or analogies
- Pattern interrupts (change your tone, pace, or position)
- Reveal something counterintuitive about your market
- Use a physical prop or visual that breaks expectations
- Ask an unexpected question that reframes the conversation
