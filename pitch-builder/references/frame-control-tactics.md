# Frame Control Tactics Reference

Detailed tactics for handling frame collisions during a pitch. Read this when the user needs deeper guidance on specific frame situations.

## The 5 Laws of Frames

1. Everyone uses frames whether they realize it or not
2. Every social encounter brings different frames together
3. Frames do not coexist -- they crash into each other
4. Only one frame survives. The stronger frame always absorbs the weaker.
5. The winning frame governs the social interaction

## How to Know Who Holds the Frame

- When you are reacting to the other person = they own the frame
- When they are reacting to you = you own the frame
- If you have to explain your authority, power, or advantage, you do NOT hold the stronger frame
- Rational appeals never win frame collisions

## Power Frame Counters

The Power Frame shows up as arrogance, lack of interest, rudeness, imperial behavior, ego-driven dominance.

Tactics:
- Never observe power rituals (no fawning, no deference, no meaningless small talk)
- Use small acts of denial and defiance COMBINED with humor
- Example: Target reaches for your materials. Pull them back: "Not yet. You have to wait for this."
- Example: Target says "I only have 15 minutes." You say: "That's okay, I only have 12." Then smile. But be serious.
- Once you take the frame, move the meeting forward in YOUR direction immediately

## Time Frame Counters

The Time Frame shows up as attention fading, body language signaling boredom, someone saying "Let's wrap this up."

Tactics:
- Never let someone else impose their time frame on you
- If attention is dying, YOU end it first: "Hey, looks like time is up. I've got to wrap this and get to my next meeting."
- Never talk faster to cram more in (shows weakness and desperation)
- Running long shows neediness
- Break incoming time frames with a prize frame: "No, I don't work like that. I need to know: are you good to work with? Can you keep appointments?"

## Analyst Frame Counters

The Analyst Frame shows up as deep drill-downs into technical details, spreadsheet obsession, "What are the numbers?" demands.

Tactics:
- Humans cannot have hot cognitions (desire, excitement) and cold cognitions (analysis, problem-solving) at the same time
- Give a high-level summary, answer directly, then redirect: "Revenue is $80M, expenses $62M, net $18M. You can verify later. Right now: are we a good fit?"
- Deploy your intrigue story
- Keep the target focused on the relationship, not the spreadsheet

## Prize Frame (Universal Counter)

Works against all opposing frames. Core concept: you are the prize, they are the commodity.

Basics:
1. Make the buyer qualify to you: "Can you tell me more about yourself? I'm picky about who I work with."
2. Protect your status -- don't let the buyer change the agenda, meeting time, or who attends
3. If the key decision maker doesn't show: "I can wait 15 minutes, but then I have to leave." Then actually leave.

Advanced:
1. Never use trial closes ("So, are we in the ballpark?") -- this shows desperation
2. Issue challenges: "So many buyers, yet only one of me."
3. Make the target perform a legitimate task to earn the deal
4. Internalize: money is a commodity. It's available in a thousand places. There is only one YOU.

Prize Frame Openers:
- "I'm glad I could find the time to meet with you today."
- "Remind me again why in the world I want to do business with you?"

## The Intrigue Story Pattern

Pre-prepare ONE personal story to deploy when the analyst frame attacks. Same story works on every audience.

The story must contain 6 elements:
1. Brief and relevant to your pitch
2. You at the center
3. Risk, danger, and uncertainty
4. Time pressure (a clock is ticking)
5. Tension (you're trying to do something but a force blocks you)
6. Serious consequences (failure will not be pretty)

Critical: tell only PART of the story. Do NOT reveal the ending. Go right back into your pitch. The unfinished narrative creates a dopamine spike. Complete the story AFTER your pitch is finished.

The "Man in the Jungle" Formula:
1. Put a man in the jungle (difficult situation)
2. Have beasts attack him (conflict, tension, obstacles)
3. Will he get to safety? (hold him at the edge -- don't resolve it)

## Status and Situational Power

Two types of status:
- Global Status: Fixed (wealth, popularity, power). Hard to change.
- Situational Status: Fluid. Changes with environment. CAN be seized.

How to seize situational status:
1. Politely ignore power rituals and avoid beta traps
2. Be unaffected by the customer's global status
3. Perpetrate small denials and defiances
4. Move the discussion into YOUR area of domain expertise
5. Apply a prize frame
6. Make the customer qualify themselves: "Remind me again why I should want to do business with you?"

Beta Traps to avoid:
- The lobby (sign in, take a seat, visitor badge)
- The conference room (wait for the "important people")
- Public spaces (coffee shops, trade show booths)
- Any ritual that reinforces someone else's status
