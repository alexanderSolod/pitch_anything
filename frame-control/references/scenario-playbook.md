# Scenario Playbook

Common real-world scenarios and how to handle them using frame control. Read this when the user describes a specific situation and you need detailed tactical guidance.

## Scenario: Investor Meeting Where They Make You Wait

**Setup:** You arrive on time. You're told to sign in, take a visitor badge, sit in the lobby. After 15 minutes, an assistant walks you to a conference room. You wait another 10 minutes. The investor arrives, barely acknowledges you, checks their phone.

**What's happening:** Classic beta trap sequence combined with a power frame. Every step is designed to establish their dominance.

**Counter sequence:**
1. Don't sit in the lobby. Stand near the entrance, look busy on your phone. When the assistant comes, say "Great, thanks" and walk with purpose, not behind them.
2. In the conference room, don't sit in the guest chair. Choose a different seat. Rearrange something small (move a water bottle, adjust the thermostat). This is a micro-denial.
3. When the investor arrives and barely acknowledges you: stand up, make strong eye contact, and say "Hey, good to see you. I've got another meeting across town, so let's get right into this. I'll give you the big idea in about 15 minutes." You've just set the time frame AND shown you're in demand.
4. If they check their phone: pause your pitch. Wait silently. When they look up, smile and say "You want me to wait?" with light humor. Then continue as if nothing happened.

## Scenario: Client Demands Detailed Financials Mid-Pitch

**Setup:** You're 8 minutes into your pitch. The CFO interrupts: "Can you walk us through the unit economics line by line? I need to see the margin breakdown by quarter."

**What's happening:** Analyst frame attack. If you comply, you'll spend 30 minutes in a spreadsheet and lose the emotional momentum of your pitch.

**Counter sequence:**
1. Acknowledge briefly: "Great question. Gross margins are 68%, improving to 74% by Q3 as we scale. The full model is in the deck I'll leave with you."
2. Redirect to relationship: "But the numbers only matter if we're the right partners. Let me tell you what happened when we launched our pilot..." [launch intrigue story]
3. If they push again: "I appreciate the rigor. That tells me you're serious. Let's do this: I'll walk you through the complete model in a follow-up. Today I want to make sure we're aligned on vision."

## Scenario: Prospect Says "We're Talking to Three Other Vendors"

**What's happening:** Commoditization frame. They're trying to reduce you to a line item in a comparison chart.

**Counter with prize frame:**
1. "That makes sense. We're also evaluating whether this is the right engagement for us. Not every partnership is worth our time."
2. "Out of curiosity, what are you actually looking for? Because if it's just the cheapest option, we're probably not your people."
3. "I'd be surprised if those conversations look anything like this one. But that's for you to figure out."

## Scenario: Meeting Gets Cut Short

**Setup:** Ten minutes in, someone says "Hey, we're going to have to cut this short. Something came up."

**What's happening:** Time frame seizure. If you panic and try to speed through your content, you lose.

**Counter sequence:**
1. Immediately: "No problem. I was going to suggest we wrap up anyway. Let me leave you with the one thing that matters..." [deliver your single strongest point]
2. "I'll send over the details. But honestly, the next step depends on whether you're serious about this. Are you?"
3. Stand up first. Pack your things. You are leaving on your terms, not theirs.

## Scenario: They Try to Renegotiate Terms After Agreement

**Setup:** You had a verbal agreement on terms. In the follow-up meeting, they say "We've been thinking, and we need to adjust the equity split / pricing / timeline."

**What's happening:** Power frame combined with a test of your neediness. If you cave immediately, they'll keep pushing.

**Counter sequence:**
1. Stay silent for 3 seconds. Let the request hang in the air.
2. "Interesting. What changed since our last conversation?"
3. Listen to their answer. Then: "I hear you. Here's the thing: we agreed on those terms because they reflect the value we're bringing. If the terms change, the scope changes. Want me to walk you through what that looks like?"
4. If they push hard: "Look, I want this to work. But I'm not going to start a partnership by undermining the agreement we already made. That's not how we operate. Are we still aligned on values here?" [Moral authority frame]

## Scenario: Being Introduced as the Junior Person

**Setup:** You're at a dinner or meeting. The host introduces you after everyone else, with less enthusiasm, or with a qualifier like "And this is [name], they're helping with the project."

**What's happening:** Status assignment. You've been positioned as beta before you even speak.

**Counter sequence:**
1. Don't correct the introduction directly (that looks defensive)
2. In your first statement, demonstrate domain expertise: "Thanks. I actually led the [impressive project/result] -- that's what brought us to this conversation."
3. Redirect attention to a question that puts you in control: "I'm curious, [senior person], how are you thinking about [complex topic in your domain]?"
4. Move quickly to your area of expertise. The longer you stay in general social territory, the harder it is to reset status.

## Scenario: Prospect Goes Silent After Your Proposal

**Setup:** You sent a proposal. It's been a week. No response to email or calls.

**What's happening:** The instinct is to chase. Send follow-ups. Offer discounts. This is neediness and it will kill the deal.

**Counter with anti-neediness (Tao of Steve):**
1. Want nothing. Do not send a "just checking in" email.
2. Be excellent. Send them something of value that has nothing to do with the deal (a relevant article, an introduction, market intel).
3. Withdraw. If you do send one follow-up, make it about your timeline, not theirs: "Hey, we're moving forward with commitments for Q2. If this isn't the right time, no worries at all. Let me know either way by Friday so I can plan accordingly."
4. The line "no worries at all" combined with a deadline is the anti-neediness sweet spot.

## General Principles for Any Scenario

1. **Identify the frame first.** Don't react until you know what frame you're facing.
2. **Never respond from a position of need.** If you feel desperate, wait. Desperation leaks through every word.
3. **Humor disarms everything.** A well-timed joke during a frame collision signals confidence and breaks tension.
4. **Physical movement matters.** Standing up, moving to a whiteboard, adjusting your position all signal frame ownership.
5. **Silence is a weapon.** After making a strong statement, stop talking. Let the other person fill the silence.
6. **Always have an exit.** If you can't credibly leave, you can't credibly negotiate. Always Be Leaving.
